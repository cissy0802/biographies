Biography: Akira Kurosawa

1910 — 1998 · aged 88
Film director · Screenwriter · The perfectionist his peers called "The Emperor"
What he most has to teach is something his elder brother said to him, a boy of thirteen, standing before the rubble: "Don't shut your eyes." It became the method of his whole life — to look straight at corpses, at failure, at himself. He carried Japanese cinema onto the world stage, yet at sixty-one slit his wrists in a failed suicide, and at sixty-five rose again to win an Oscar. But the coin has a glaring other side: the "Emperor" who could reverse a river's course and burn a castle down for a single shot used that same intensity to drive his finest collaborator into sixteen years of silence.

◆Life in Brief

Born in Tokyo in 1910 into a family of samurai descent. During the Great Kanto Earthquake of 1923, his eldest brother Heigo walked the 13-year-old through streets strewn with corpses, forcing him to keep his eyes open — a scene that shaped him. Heigo, a benshi (live narrator of silent films) and his gateway into cinema, took his own life in 1933. Kurosawa joined P.C.L. (later absorbed into Toho) as an assistant director in 1936 under Kajiro Yamamoto, and directed his first film, Sanshiro Sugata, in 1943. In 1950 Rashomon was sent to Venice without his knowledge and won the Golden Lion in 1951, opening the Western world to Japanese cinema for the first time. Seven Samurai (1954), Throne of Blood, and Yojimbo then cemented his mastery. He left Toho in the 1960s to strike out on his own, met setback after setback, and in 1971 attempted suicide. He was revived by the Soviet-backed Dersu Uzala (1975), and in his late years — bankrolled by Lucas, Coppola, and French capital — made Kagemusha (1980) and Ran (1985). Thirty films in all; an honorary Academy Award in 1990; death in 1998.

◆Key Decision: Seven Samurai (1954), refusing to cut it though three times over budget

Seven Samurai was planned to shoot in a few months; it took nearly a year and ran to roughly three times its budget, the most expensive Japanese film to date. Toho twice ordered the cameras stopped. Kurosawa's response was a kind of gamble: he did not argue — he went fishing. He was betting that the studio had already sunk in too much to abandon it. As he later explained, an incomplete masterpiece is a worse loss than a finished mediocrity. The producers eventually swallowed the cost and let him finish.

More fundamental was his stubbornness about the cut: told to trim the three-and-a-half-hour film to two hours, he flatly refused. He believed its rhythm — from the loose recruitment to the storm-lashed final battle — only worked whole. Today Seven Samurai is counted among the greatest films ever made, and directly spawned Hollywood's The Magnificent Seven. The lesson is not "overrunning is fine," but that his judgment about a work's integrity outweighed his fear of the short-term ledger — and that he forced the room to do it through action, not argument.

Sources: Stuart Galbraith IV, The Emperor and the Wolf (2001); Donald Richie, The Films of Akira Kurosawa (1965/1998).

◆Turning Point: December 22, 1971 — crawling back from a Soviet forest after the razor

The late 1960s were Kurosawa's darkest trough. In 1968 he was hired by 20th Century Fox to direct the Japanese half of Tora! Tora! Tora!, and was fired a few weeks in — cited for a runaway schedule and suspected instability. For "the Emperor," it was a humiliation. In 1970 his first color film, Dodesukaden, flopped. He was now seen as washed-up and too expensive; almost no one in Japan would fund him.

On December 22, 1971, the 61-year-old Kurosawa cut his wrists and throat dozens of times with a razor, and survived the attempt. He sank into a deeper silence. The turn came from an unexpected direction: Mosfilm invited him to Siberia to shoot Dersu Uzala. In 1975 that Russian-language film won the Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film. A man written off as finished at home had proven, in a foreign forest, that he could still direct. The trough's lesson: an artist's "end" is usually a verdict passed by others; refuse the verdict, and the exit may lie somewhere you never thought to look.

Sources: Stuart Galbraith IV, The Emperor and the Wolf (2001); Kurosawa, Something Like an Autobiography (1982).

◆Character & Habits: painting every frame, firing real arrows at his actors

When the money wasn't there, Kurosawa painted. For Kagemusha and Ran he made hundreds of watercolors and oils by hand — the composition, color, and light of every scene, worked out first on paper (later published as an album). His method was to "paint" the entire film before shooting it. When financing dragged, the paintings were both design and a way of keeping himself upright.

His control of detail was near-obsessive, which earned him the nickname "The Emperor." For a single shot he had a whole stream re-routed; rooftops out of frame were still torn down, because "knowing it's there is wrong"; for a scene in Ran he really built a castle and burned it. Even the backs of drawers and places an actor would never open were dressed to period.

Most extreme is the ending of Throne of Blood (1957): in the scene where Toshiro Mifune is killed by a hail of arrows, the arrows were real, fired by expert archers just off-frame to thud into the wood behind him. The genuine terror on Mifune's face was drawn out by real arrows. Kurosawa also pioneered shooting with three cameras and telephoto lenses at once, so actors never knew which was rolling — capturing the most natural performance.

Sources: Kurosawa, Something Like an Autobiography (1982); Donald Richie, The Films of Akira Kurosawa.

◆Controversy & Shadow: the Emperor's price — burned money, driven people, and the break with Mifune

First, extravagance and autocracy cost him his own country's trust. "The Emperor" was not only an honorific but a jab. His budgets repeatedly blew out, his schedules stretched without limit, and after the 1960s Japanese studios treated him as radioactive. His late epics were nearly all made on foreign capital — Lucas, Coppola, French producers. A national-treasure director unable to raise money at home is the bill for his arrogance and perfectionism.

Second, a sixteen-year cold war with Toshiro Mifune. Mifune was his golden partner across 16 films; the two all but defined each other. But Red Beard (1965) shot for nearly two years, and the contract forced Mifune to keep his beard the whole time, unable to take other work, at heavy cost. After that film, they never worked together again. The complexity and resentment in Mifune's later remarks about Kurosawa are the sharpest footnote to the "Emperor and the Wolf" legend — great work paid for with a partner's consumed years.

Third, the charge of being "too Western." Japanese critics — especially the New Wave generation around Nagisa Oshima — attacked Kurosawa as "a director who makes films for foreigners," too theatrical, too un-Japanese. His reputation abroad at times exceeded his standing at home. Together with the depression his 1971 suicide attempt lays bare, it shows that this "strong man's" inner life was far more fragile than the heroes on his screen.

Sources: Stuart Galbraith IV, The Emperor and the Wolf (2001); Donald Richie, The Films of Akira Kurosawa.

◆Quotes & Sources

  • "If you shut your eyes to a frightening sight, you end up being frightened. If you look at everything straight on, there is nothing to be afraid of." His brother Heigo's words, recounted in Something Like an Autobiography (1982).
  • "I am not especially strong. I am not especially gifted. I simply do not like to lose." — Something Like an Autobiography (1982)
  • "To be an artist means never to avert one's eyes." Attributed to Kurosawa; heard in Chris Marker's documentary A.K. (1985).
  • "I don't feel that I have yet grasped the essence of cinema. … I hope that by continuing to work, I will understand what cinema is." — Honorary Academy Award acceptance speech, March 1990

◆What BigCat Can Take Away

Kurosawa's most transferable idea is the method of "don't shut your eyes": whether it's failure, criticism, or your own shadow, looking straight at it costs less than avoiding it, because avoidance keeps the fear draining you. The second is "paint it first" — when resources and timing weren't there, he ran the entire work through on paper as storyboards, which was both design and an anchor that kept the vision from distorting over a long wait. For anyone chasing the "AI super-individual," this is close to a discipline of prototyping the whole system in a cheap medium before committing to costly execution. But the third is a warning bell: his perfectionism built masterpieces and also burned trust and drove off his best partner. You can be the Emperor toward the work, never toward people. Press your standard onto living people past a certain point, and what you lose is not money — it's the collaborator who might have stayed with you for sixteen years.

◆Questions to Sit With

If Kurosawa lived today, would he still need "Emperor"-style total control?
Today's virtual production and AI storyboarding could replace his money-burning insistence on shooting things for real: the re-routed river, the burned castle could all be tried and discarded in a virtual scene, sparing him money and enemies. But turn it around: how much of the sheer "realness" in his images — real arrows, real fire, a gaze that stares at the dead — comes precisely from the irreversible, expensive act of shooting it for real? When everything can be undone and redone, does the artist lose the very necessity of pushing himself to the edge? Technology lowers cost, but may also dilute the force of the all-or-nothing bet itself.
Are the conditions of his success — the big-studio system, a loyal fixed crew — still reproducible?
Kurosawa relied on a stable "unit": the same cinematographers, art directors, and actors (above all Mifune) working together again and again into deep rapport. This workshop-style, long-term arrangement has all but dissolved in today's project-based, gig-ified film industry. How much of his genius was personal, and how much grew out of that system? It reminds every "super-individual" that the ceiling of the work is often set by whether you can sustain a high-trust, long-term circle of collaboration — and Kurosawa, precisely there, both benefited from it and destroyed it with his own hands.
The "too Western" charge — bias, or a blind spot?
His homeland accused him of making films for foreigners; the world crowned him a master — the rift itself is worth turning over. To be seen by the world an artist often pays the price of being "seen as a traitor" by their own people, because worldliness usually means distilling out some of the local texture only insiders understand. Was this his blind spot (something genuinely lost), or his compatriots' narrowness (treating national character as the only standard)? Possibly both — and anyone attempting cross-cultural expression should be ready to bear both gazes at once.
