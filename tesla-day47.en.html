Biography: Nikola Tesla

1856 — 1943 · aged 86
Inventor of the AC system · pioneer of radio and wireless power · a genius who died in a hotel room
He gave humanity the alternating current that flows from every wall socket today, yet died alone and buried in debt in a New York hotel, with only a few pigeons for company. Tesla was an extreme fusion of technical genius and commercial ineptitude: he could run a fully working motor in his mind before ever building it, yet tore up with his own hands a patent contract that would have made him fabulously rich. His life forces a brutal question — are inventing something world-changing and living well off it really the same skill?

◆Life in Brief

Born in 1856 in the village of Smiljan in the Austrian Empire (today Croatia), an ethnic Serb whose father was an Orthodox priest. He studied engineering at the Graz Polytechnic but left without a degree. Strolling through Budapest in 1882, he claimed to suddenly "see" in his mind the complete design of a rotating magnetic field — the heart of the AC motor. In 1884 he emigrated to America with a few coins in his pocket, joined Edison's company, and soon broke with him over a dispute about pay. Between 1887 and 1888 he built the polyphase AC induction motor; George Westinghouse bought the patents, igniting the "war of currents" against Edison, champion of direct current. The 1893 Chicago World's Fair and the 1895 Niagara Falls hydroelectric plant both adopted AC, and Tesla's system won the entire modern power grid. He then turned to wireless power, building the Wardenclyffe Tower in 1901; it collapsed after funding dried up and Marconi got there first. His later years grew lonelier and poorer; he claimed to have built a "death ray," and in 1943 he died alone in the Hotel New Yorker.

◆Key Decisions: Quitting on Principle, Handing Over an Empire, Tearing Up His Own Fortune

First, in 1885 he left Edison over a single promise. As the story goes, Edison offered $50,000 if he could improve the DC dynamos. Tesla toiled for months and succeeded; when he asked for the money, Edison reportedly said, "Tesla, you don't understand our American humor" — it had only been a joke. Whatever the exact details, the outcome is certain: he resigned in anger. A newly arrived immigrant with almost nothing chose to walk out rather than be toyed with. That rupture set him on a collision course with direct current.

Second, in 1888 he sold his AC patents to Westinghouse rather than build his own empire. He judged that what he wanted was for AC to blanket the world, not to run a company himself. Westinghouse bought his polyphase system for roughly $60,000 in cash and stock, plus a royalty of $2.50 per horsepower. The choice gave AC an industrial-scale engine for expansion — but also handed his fate to someone else.

Third, around 1897, he tore up that royalty contract. The "war of currents" had drained Westinghouse's finances, and investors pressed him to shed the heavy Tesla royalty or the company would fall. By Westinghouse's own account, Tesla tore the contract to shreds in front of him, saying: you believed in me when it counted; now it is my turn to save you. What he gave up, judged by the installed capacity of the day, was a sum that would have made him one of the richest men alive. He preserved the standard-bearer of AC — and shut the door on his own financial freedom.

Sources: W. Bernard Carlson, Tesla: Inventor of the Electrical Age (2013); Marc Seifer, Wizard: The Life and Times of Nikola Tesla (1996).

◆Career Turning Point: Wardenclyffe Tower — From "Father of the Wireless Age" to a Bankrupt Ruin

Having won the grid, Tesla aimed his ambition at something larger: transmitting power and information across the globe without wires. In 1901 he persuaded the financier J.P. Morgan to invest about $150,000 in a 57-meter tower at Wardenclyffe on Long Island. What he sold Morgan was wireless communication; what he truly wanted to build was "free wireless power." That same year, Marconi completed a transatlantic radio transmission with far cruder equipment — snatching away the laurel Tesla felt was his, and draining the investor's confidence.

When Tesla admitted his real goal was wireless power that no one could meter, Morgan flatly refused to add funds — a business that cannot charge means nothing to a banker. The tower was abandoned and demolished for debt in 1917. This was the watershed of his life: before it he stood victorious at the frontier of his age; for nearly forty years after it he had not a single successful major project, sliding into poverty, isolation, and obscurity. His late pronouncements grew ever grander — a particle-beam "death ray," communication with Mars — while his achievements grew ever fewer.

Sources: Carlson, Tesla (2013); Seifer, Wizard (1996).

◆Character & Habits: Building Machines in His Head, Everything Divisible by 3, Feeding Pigeons at the End

He could design and "run" machines entirely in his mind. By his own account, he needed no drawings to conceive an invention; he could let a device turn in his imagination for weeks, inspect where it wore down, and refine it until it was perfect before ever building it. This almost supernatural visualization was the core of how he worked.

He had severe compulsive tendencies: everything he did had to be divisible by three — walking three times around a block before entering, wiping his cutlery with a fresh set of 18 napkins at dinner. He loathed touching hair, pearls, and round objects, dined alone, slept very little (reportedly only about two hours a night), and remained lifelong celibate, pouring all his energy into invention.

In his last years he gave his affection almost entirely to pigeons. He fed them daily in the park and loved one white pigeon above all, saying he loved it "as a man loves a woman," and that when it died he felt his life's work had died with it. The man who bent the forces of nature to his will was left, in the end, with a bird as his softest tie.

Sources: Tesla, My Inventions (1919); Carlson, Tesla (2013).

◆Controversies & Shadows: A Businessman with No Instinct, Runaway Boasts, the Stain of Eugenics

First, a catastrophic sense for business and people. Again and again he handed his wealth to others or gave it up outright — unable to protect his interests, poor at running things, and in his last years living on hotel bills that Westinghouse paid out of old loyalty. The genius of invention and the incompetence of finance were taken to an extreme in him, a reminder that creating value and keeping value are two entirely different abilities.

Second, he grew more prophet than engineer. From the 1930s he frequently announced astonishing inventions to the press: a "death ray"/teleforce weapon that could down aircraft, signals possibly from Mars, unlimited energy. Most were never demonstrated in verifiable form, blurring the line between vision and delusion and eroding public trust in him.

Third, the stain of eugenics. In a 1935 magazine article he predicted that by 2100 eugenics would be widespread, advocating that the "unfit" be prevented from breeding to "purify" humanity. Such views were not unique in his era, but they read as repellent today — even the most dazzling mind can be captured by the darkest prejudices of its time.

Sources: Tesla, "A Machine to End War," Liberty magazine (1935); Seifer, Wizard (1996).

◆Quotes & Sources

  • "Let the future tell the truth, and evaluate each one according to his work and accomplishments." — Carried in a Scientific American tribute (c. 1919)
  • "I do not think there is any thrill that can go through the human heart like that felt by the inventor as he sees some creation of the brain unfolding to success." — Autobiography, My Inventions (1919)
  • "If your hate could be turned into electricity, it would light up the whole world." — Widely attributed to Tesla

◆Takeaways for BigCat

Tesla is the most brilliant — and most painful — specimen of the "super-individual." The brilliance: one man could complete the design and debugging of an entire system in his head, almost "replacing physical trial-and-error with mental simulation" — precisely the leverage you seek, amplified to the extreme in the AI age. But his tragedy is what deserves to be nailed to your wall: he treated "creating value" and "capturing value" as one and the same, and managed only the former. He tore up contracts, gave away patents, ignored the logic of metering — so the world enjoyed his inventions while he himself died in debt. For anyone who would move the world by personal talent, this is the sharpest lesson: however extreme your technical peak, without a resource structure (cash flow, equity, a chargeable model) that lets you keep funding the next invention, even the greatest contribution will run dry in your hands. The second lesson is focus: his late years were pulled apart by too many grand visions, sliding from "building AC" to "announcing a death ray" — it is the part you can verify and deliver that separates you from a mere dreamer.

◆Questions Worth Sitting With

If Tesla were born today, would he be the next Musk, or still die destitute?
Today's venture capital and patent systems might capture value for him — a "Tesla" with partners to hold down the business side could become a titan. But look at it the other way: it was precisely his indifference to money and metering that let him dare to imagine "free wireless power," a gloriously anti-commercial vision. Fit him into a structure that must answer to investors and you might make him rich, but you might grind away that reckless disregard for gain. A genius and their flaw are often the same coin.
Tearing up the Westinghouse contract — was that nobility, or a self-destructive naïveté?
Reading it as pure generosity is too romantic. More likely: what Tesla truly cared about was that AC win, not that he grow rich; money to him was fuel for building the next thing, not the goal. The problem is that when you refuse to accumulate any resources for yourself, you mortgage your very ability to keep helping the world to someone else's willingness to keep feeding you. He preserved AC but lost the capital to be the next Tesla — altruism with no margin for self-preservation snuffs out its own future sparks.
What actually draws the line between "vision" and "delusion"?
Tesla's early "madness" (wireless communication, remote control) mostly came true; his late "death ray" and "Martian signals" became a laughingstock. The difference may lie not in how bold the idea is, but in whether a path to verification is still in hand — with a reproducible experiment or a deliverable prototype, wild ideas are merely ahead of their time; with only pronouncements and no artifact, they slide into delusion. That offers everyone at the frontier a self-check: can my boldest idea take its next step down to something others can actually test?
